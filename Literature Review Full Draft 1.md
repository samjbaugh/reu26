**Literature Review**

**Introduction**

Growing Season Length and Diurnal Temperature Range are impactful atmospheric and climatological factors for both society and agriculture across the world. The length of the frost-free season informs the planting and harvest decisions of farmers, with widespread effects on agricultural yields, average food price, and levels of food scarcity in regions where agriculture is challenged by water availability or temperature extremes. Diurnal Temperature Range is considered a measure of extreme climate change and has widespread impacts on human health, disease transmission, agricultural productivity, and ecological systems (Liu et al., 2026). This project seeks to better understand variability within these two statistics by considering whether their variability is changing with time, and, if any have significant impacts, what atmospheric or geographic variables are responsible for that change.

**Key Topics, Definitions, and Methodology**

	The two overarching key topics discussed in this paper are Growing Season Length (GSL) and Diurnal Temperature Range (DTR). GSL is defined as the number of calendar days between the Last Spring Frost (LSF) \- the final calendar date that surface temperature reaches below 0°C in the spring \- and First Fall Frost (FFF) \- the first calendar date that surface temperature reaches below 0°C in the fall. DTR is defined as the difference between the daily maximum surface temperature, and the daily minimum surface temperature. When increases and decreases in DTR are mentioned, further context will be provided to indicate whether either daily maximum or minimum temperatures are more responsible for that change.

While acquiring and collating sources for the literature review, scientific articles and papers were found through a combination of Google Scholar keyword searches, citations of other included publications, or lists of new publications citing other included publications. Each paper was first individually read and then analyzed for bullet pointed lists on key points, methods, conclusions, limitations, and open questions, before those notes were transferred into prose for use in the final literature review.

**Growing Season Length**

***Overarching Trends***

	The overwhelming consensus among published literature is that GSL across both the entire contiguous United States (US) and the Great Lakes region has increased in recent decades (see, e.g., Kunkel et al, 2004; Yu et al, 2014; McCabe et al, 2015). This consensus of GSL increase since 1980 (see, e.g., Kunkel; Yu) aligns with the observed rapid increase in global mean surface temperature commencing the late 1970s and early 1980s (Lindsey et al, 2025). 

Also noted by Kunkel (2004) are the spatial differences in the linear fits for GSL, with the least-squares linear fit for the western US showing an increase of 19 days century\-1 while the linear fit for the eastern US shows an increase of only 3 days century\-1. Kunkel includes that these differences are a reflection of the difference in observed increase in mean temperature between the western and eastern contiguous US, and shows that mean temperature anomaly is positively correlated with GSL anomaly across the US. 

	Additionally, both Kunkel (2004) and Brown (2026) conduct analysis on which season has a greater influence on the lengthening of GSL. Kunkel (2004) notes that while GSL increased in length by 5-10 days from 1980 to 2000, average LSF date was about 4 days earlier and average FFF was about 2 days later than the 1895-2000 average, indicating that changes to LSF had a greater impact on GSL lengthening. Brown (2026) takes a different approach, considering the concentration of freeze days and defining freeze day concentration periods containing 75% of freeze days. Their analysis found that, within the contiguous US, 327 counties showed significant trends towards later starts of their freeze day concentration periods, while 735 counties showed significant trends towards earlier end dates of their freeze day concentration periods. While Brown’s analysis did not exclude counties from being included in both categories, and the metric does not specifically analyze the final day below the inclusive 0°C threshold as used to define GSL, LSF, and FFF, the higher quantity of counties exhibiting significant trends towards an earlier end to the period of time containing 75% of their freeze days indicates that this analysis to some extent agrees with Kunkel (2004) in concluding that earlier LSF has a larger impact on increasing GSL than later FFF.

	Overarching trends in GSL are not the primary focus of this new work, given the consensus presented from the existing body of published works. However, given the commonly-held misconception that trends towards warmer mean temperatures indicate greater variability in the extremes nearly all atmospheric phenomena, it is important to establish a background understanding of the increasing trend in mean temperature and GSL before analyzing the variability in GSL to better determine its causes and any present changes.

***Variability***

	Recent research on GSL variability has analyzed the impacts of a large group of covariates, including climate indices, atmospheric teleconnections, oceanic and coastal factors, and Northern Hemisphere jet stream position. (see, e.g., Strong & McCabe 2017; McCabe et al, 2015; Dobson et al, 2020; Cornish et al, 2026; Hudson et al, 2022; and Thomas and Bosilovich 2026\) 

Despite the large body of work, there is notably less consensus in the community on impacts of climate indices than on other covariates or more overarching trends. For example, McCabe (2015) argues the correlations between detrended LSF and spring climate indices and between FFF and detrended fall climate indices are both weak, noting that most of the correlations found are not significant at  *p* \< 0.05, and concluding that the most statistically significant correlations to be found for the contiguous US are the positive correlations of the Atlantic Multidecadal Oscillation (AMO) with LSF and FFF. 

On a more regional scale, Yu (2014) found that interannual variation of the FFF and GSL appear to have a 3.5 year period relating to the El Nino Southern Oscillation (ENSO), and the variation of the LSF has a shorter 2.5 year period appearing to be related to the North Atlantic Oscillation (NAO). This contradiction is less directly disagreeing with the work of McCabe (2015) as it is considering that certain climate indices have impacts on a regional scale that might not be coherent and detectable in a national analysis such as McCabe’s. 

Meanwhile, in a more recent analysis of the entire contiguous US, Thomas and Bosilovich (2026) argue that the East Pacific/North Pacific pattern (EP/NP) is significantly correlated with timing of both LSF and FFF in most regions of the country. While they leave open for further research understanding the causes of how the EP/NP influences spring and fall temperatures, they do note that its influence on GSL, LSF, and FFF is through its influence on seasonal temperatures – as previously established by Kunkel (2004) when correlating mean temperature anomalies with GSL anomalies. Thomas and Bosilovich also connect this influence on temperature and, by extension, GSL, to the commonly identifiable circulation patterns over the US caused by positive and negative phases of the EP/NP which create favorable conditions for advection to play a strong role in impacting temperature, and also note that the circulation associated with the EP/NP was similar to the pattern identified by Strong and McCabe (2017).

Interestingly, at the time when Strong and McCabe (2017) identified the aforementioned pattern in their work, they labeled it as being similar to the negative polarity of the Pacific-North American Oscillation (PNA) rather than either polarity of the EP/NP as a result of the pattern exhibiting three or more centers of action and originating in the Pacific Ocean. They concluded that this pattern accounted for 29% of variation in LSF across the western US, and that in combination with other atmospheric teleconnections– some of which were notably not aligned with the PNA but not connected with any other indice at the time of publication in 2017–  and circulation patterns, they could explain anywhere from 25-48% of the variability found in LSF and FFF across the US. However, they were unable to conclude with certainty that the circulations were the direct drivers of variance, given the possibility that the circulations simply created the conditions for anomalously low temperatures through ridging and troughing across the contiguous US.

The final publication that considered atmospheric teleconnections or circulation patterns was Hudson (2022). While their work considered the entire northern hemisphere jet stream (NHJ), their conclusions still have notable applications in the contiguous US. They, as Strong and McCabe (2017) suggested when including that they did not have the certainty necessary to assign cause for variability to the circulations themselves, concluded that the main impact of circulation patterns associated with the NHJ was the impact of its anomalous positions on surface meteorology at key moments in the growing season. They concluded that northern positions of the NHJ generally affected more regions than southern positions, and those northern positions typically corresponded with increased GSL when they occurred in the spring, and decreased GSL when they occurred in the fall. Also included in Hudson’s conclusions was a note that northern NHJ position anomalies in the fall generally produced more significant responses in GSL than springtime northern anomalies. Through Hudson (2022), Strong and McCabe (2017), and Thomas and Bolisovich (2026)’s work, there does appear to be a general consensus that while atmospheric teleconnections and circulations do not have a direct impact on GSL variability, their impact on temperature through anomalous ridging and troughing extends to impact the variability of GSL as well as temperature due to the direct relationship found between them.

The final covariate frequently analyzed for impact on GSL variability was proximity to the large bodies of water. Given water’s specific heat capacity being higher than air and the resulting lag of changes to temperature of bodies of water behind the associated air temperature changes, causing phenomena such as sea and land breezes, it could reasonably be expected that proximity to a large body of water might have some impact on variability in GSL. Dobson (2020) placed their focus entirely on the southern coast of Lake Erie, choosing sites in northeastern Ohio and northwestern Pennsylvania, with the intent of determining what effects the lake had on GSL and GSL variability. They concluded that average FFF was significantly later for coastal regions than inland regions – up to 15-20 days difference – but that the rate of change in FFF was greater for the inland regions. However, these findings did not extend to LSF, as they found no significant difference between coastal and inland regions for that metric. While the presumption that the heat capacity of water in Lake Erie as the cause of the later FFF along the coast would be correct, it is notable that the publication concluded that the rate of increase in GSL was not significantly different between inland and coastal regions. Additionally, while considering impacts on variability, Dobson hypothesizes that the lake’s mitigation of temperature extremes might be a driver behind the slower rate of change in FFF along the coast, and one might hypothesize that the logic behind Dobson’s theory – that a lake of Lake Erie’s size could serve as a mitigating factor to temperature extremes – may also extend to the mitigation of anomalously early or late LSF and FFF dates and therefore reduce variability in GSL along coastlines overall.

As a whole, the existing body of work investigating variability in GSL has established a group of correlations which explain GSL variability to some significant degree, but notably passes over considering changes to the degree of variability itself. Understanding the general trend in GSL variability is paramount to future success in forecasting the growing season, as an increase in GSL variability would cause significantly more hazard to crop viability in the early spring and late fall portions of the growing season as anomalously late LSF dates and anomalously early FFF dates would become more common and inherently more unpredictable.

**Diurnal Temperature Range**

***Overarching Trends***

There is a surprising amount of disagreement among published work on the exact state of overarching trends in Diurnal Temperature Range, despite its importance to society both as a matter of human health and as a metric to improve understanding of the impacts of anthropogenic warming. Common logic and colloquial understanding of anthropogenic warming would have the average person assume that the increased volatility in the world's climate from warmer temperatures should cause DTR to increase across the board in a similar manner to GSL, and some papers do indicate this. Zhong (2023, as cited in Liu et al, 2026\) suggests that a recent higher warming rate of daily maximum temperature than daily minimum temperature has been detected and would imply an increase to DTR, but multiple other recent papers (see, e.g., Liu et al, 2026; Brown et al, 2026; and Cornish et al, 2026\) conclude the opposite– that DTR is in fact decreasing.

These papers have various metrics to either imply or prove this decrease, and focus on a varying set of time intervals while doing so, but the consensus conclusion was that recent decades have observed a decrease in DTR as a result of a greater magnitude of warming of daily minimum temperatures as compared to daily maximum temperatures. 

Brown (2026) comes to this conclusion on an annual timescale through their observations that 99% of contiguous US land area observed statistically significant warming to annual average minimum temperatures, while only 79% of contiguous US land area observed a statistically significant warming in annual average maximum temperatures. 

Cornish (2026) notes that across New Jersey and the surrounding areas, the average increase in the frequency of “extremely warm” minimum temperatures was greater than the increase in “extremely hot” maximum temperature. They supposed that the more rapid warming of minimum temperature was linked to an increase in the presence of atmospheric moisture that boosts cloud cover and soil moisture, causing nighttime temperatures to maintain a higher temperature.

Liu (2026) takes the research one step further, distinguishing trends in DTR by season, and identifying the outsized role of the Winter and Spring seasons in decreasing DTR as compared to the impact of Summer and Autumn. They hypothesize that Summer and Autumn have a smaller role in the overall decrease because the impact of clouds is more pronounced in these seasons– that is, since soil moisture is lower during these seasons in the Northern Hemisphere, temperature is more dependent on the amount of incoming solar radiation and therefore the decrease in DTR is of a lesser magnitude as a result of having fewer moderating factors.

While Huang (2023) at first appears to break with the general consensus of the previously mentioned articles with their claim that DTR is in fact increasing 0.091 \+/- 0.008 °C decade\-1, it is important to note that their work considers the entirety of the Northern Hemisphere rather than just the contiguous US, and their individual rate of change for just North America (Huang et al, 2023, Figure 1c) indicates their agreement that DTR is decreasing across the continental US, as well as extending this consensus into Canada and Mexico. 

What, then, is the driver behind North America’s anomalously decreasing DTR? Given the frequent attribution of decreasing DTR in the contiguous US to the presence of moisture– or lack thereof– wouldn’t one expect that decreasing trend to also be observed in areas of the Northern Hemisphere such as the arid interior regions of Eurasia? Additionally, given the incredibly small degree to which any trend at all is occurring in North America – Huang (2023) estimates the overarching trend across the continent as a whole to be \-0.008 °C decade\-1 – which regions within the country are more responsible for that trend? Are there differing trends in DTR on the state or county level, and if so, what is the driving mechanism behind those differences?

***Variability***

Another important aspect of DTR is its variability both spatially and temporally, especially given its relation to growing season length. A more variable DTR makes predicting the LSF or FFF increasingly difficult, as small overarching trends in mean temperature in an area where the DTR is large and relatively stable is unlikely to have a large impact on growing season length, but that same trend to mean temperature in an area where the DTR is small or relatively variable could easily alter the time of LSF or FFF by weeks to months on a year to year basis.

There is very little published literature to date on year to year variability in DTR, but Wang & Dickinson (2013, via Huang et al, 2023\) note that solar radiation is directly correlated with decadal DTR variability, since an increase in clouds reflecting shortwave radiation in the day and preventing longwave reemission at night reduces maximum temperatures, increases minimum temperatures, and therefore decreases DTR. Given the general trend of increasing hydrological extremes such as droughts and floods and the associated changes in cloud cover and incoming shortwave radiation that come with those events, this correlation is notable to begin understanding how variability in DTR might change in the coming decades under anthropogenic warming.

In a very similar manner to the overarching trends in DTR as a whole, questions remain in quantifying and characterizing whether changes are underway in DTR variability as the climate changes. Are there regional or county-level changes to variability, and if so, are those changes of a large enough magnitude that they would have significant impacts on GSL and GSL variability as well?

**Conclusion**

Growing Season Length and Diurnal Temperature Range are, for good reason, topics that have been studied extensively in recent years for overarching trends and response to global anthropogenic warming. In general, the scientific community has found a large degree of consensus while studying these two topics. This consensus includes agreement that GSL is increasing, primarily due to advancing LSF, and that DTR is very slightly decreasing in the contiguous US as a result of a greater increase in daily minimum temperatures than daily maximum temperatures and changes to cloud cover and incoming solar radiation.

However, there is plenty left to be studied for greater understanding, especially in relation to the variability of these two metrics as anthropogenic climate change continues to alter the world around us. The literature presented has notable holes on whether there are discernable trends in GSL or DTR variability on any spatial scale, which this new work seeks to fill through statistical analysis of the variance in GSL and DTR, and preliminary work to identify and isolate the covariates responsible for any variability that may be found as the project proceeds.
