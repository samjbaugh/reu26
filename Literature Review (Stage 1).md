**Introduction**  
	In the first stage of literature review, the main focus has been placed on a small set of papers to build knowledge of previous research on the proposed project’s topic. Key definitions across these three papers include ‘frost-free season’ as the time of the year at a location where temperature stays exclusively above 0°C and ‘climate indices’ (including ENSO, PNA, AMO, AO, NAO, and PDO)– numerical representations of various regional oscillations–  as they relate to variability in CONUS spring and fall temperatures.  
In these summaries, any notes or questions of my own are within \[square brackets\] to distinguish them from the content of the papers themselves.

**Kunkel et al. 2004 \- Temporal variations in frost-free season in United States 1895-2000**

* Key Points  
  * Four distinct periods of frost-free season length trend in studied time period: decrease of \~1 week from 1895 \- 1910, increase of \~1 week from 1910 \- 1930, relative stability from 1930 \- 1980, and increase of \~1 week from 1980 \- 2000  
  * Degree of frost-free season length increase significantly higher in Western U.S. than in Eastern U.S.  
* Procedure (inc. Pros/Cons)  
  * Digitized all pre-1948 NWS COOP data for more comprehensive dataset spatially and temporally (total of 794 stations)  
  * Frost-free season defined with inclusive threshold of 0°C daily minimum temperature  
  * Station frost-free season spring start and fall end values averaged into climate divisions  
  * Climate divisions averaged into state values  
  * State values averaged with weighting for state area to derive national values  
  * Quality control:   
    * Observation considered an outlier if value was \>5 standard deviations from station’s monthly mean  
    * Confirmed change from liquid-in-glass thermometers in cotton region shelter (CRS) to Max-Min Temperature System (MMTS) did not have significant effects on nighttime temperature  
    * Confirmed urban heat island effects did not impact data.  
* Limitations  
  * Stations in low density areas to make a greater contribution to national average  
* Conclusions  
  * A linear fit for frost-free season length in the Western U.S. is 19 days per century, but only 3 days per century for the Eastern U.S.  
    * Spatial differences in frost-free season length also reflect patterns of mean annual temperature changes, with a larger change in the west than in the east.  
    * Increase in frost-free season length of \~2 weeks is a substantial change  
  * A direct relationship exists between warmer periods and longer frost-free seasons, as well as cooler periods and shorter frost-free seasons  
    * Timing of two main increases in frost-free season broadly coincides with timing of periods of increasing mean annual temperature  
    * May reflect observation that maximum temperatures are rising slower than minimum temperatures  
* Open Questions  
  * Moister air masses generally allow less solar radiation to the earth’s surface \[and warmer air is capable of holding more moisture per unit volume than cooler air\]. Are longer growing seasons associated with less global solar radiation caused by moister air masses? \[and if this is the case, what about a reduction in global solar radiation would be the key contributor to lengthening frost-free season length?\]

**Strong & McCabe, 2017 \- Observed variations in U.S. frost timing linked to atmospheric circulation patterns**

* Key Points  
  * Frost-triggering temperature extremes are associated with atmospheric circulation patterns delivering anomalously cold air masses  
    * These circulations appear responsive to historical warming, account for more frost timing variability than hemispheric or regional temperature indices  
* Procedure (inc. Pros/Cons)  
  * Partitioned contiguous U.S. into four regions for spring and four regions for fall  
    * Analyzed overall change in frost-free season for each region  
  * Analyzed impact of hemispheric warming on Spring and Fall indices  
    * Found that hemispheric warming accounted for 8-16% of spring variance and up to 27% of fall variance  
  * Calculated regional seasonal temperature indices for both spring and fall (denoted as TR)  
    * TR accounted for 44% of variation in spring frost timing in the west region  
  * Used 500 mb geopotential height to identify teleconnections with impacts on variance in spring and fall frost  
    * Found anomalously late spring frost in the North region was related to troughing over eastern North America, accounting for 32% of its variance  
      * Not correlated with the PNA index  
    * Found a ridge-trough structure over North America was related to anomalously late spring frost in the Central Region, accounting for 26% of its variance  
      * Not correlated with the PNA index  
    * Found a slightly different ridge-trough structure related to anomalously late spring frost in the south region, accounting for 38% of its variance  
      * Not correlated with the PNA index  
    * Found a pattern similar to the negative polarity of the PNA accounted for 48% of the variance in spring frost for the west region  
      * PNA index itself accounted for 29% of the variation in spring in the west region  
    * Analysis of fall frost timing found similar circulation patterns but with the opposite sign (logically, since an increase in F (later frost in fall) is associated with ridging, but an increase in S (later frost in spring) is associated with troughing  
      * While the PNA index had a statistically significant influence on the western region in the spring, it did not have a statistically significant influence on that region in the fall  
  * Varied quantity of regions analyzed from 1 to 10  
    * Circulation pattern indices accounted more for variance than regional mean or hemispheric mean temperature  
    * Correlation with circulation and temperature tended to decline as the number of regions used increased  
* Conclusions  
  * Atmospheric Teleconnections / Circulation Patterns (especially anomalous ridging and troughing) accounted for 25-48% of variability in frost-timing indices  
  * Frost-driving circulation patterns were broadly similar to the PNA (i.e. originating in the Pacific Ocean with ≥3 centers of action)  
  * Certain teleconnections (CSN, CSC, and CFC) did not align with the PNA, which indicates these are distinct from those previously mentioned.  
  * No certainty that circulations are drivers of variance other than temperature  
  * Fall patterns in all four regions analyzed correlated with hemispheric temperature, meaning they were amplified by warming  
  * Regardless of when– and for how long– the frost-free season occurs, continental scale teleconnections are likely to maintain their influence on frost timing variability  
    * Important while considering anthropogenic climate change is shown to lengthen frost-free period  
* Open Questions  
  * How will global climate models fare in representing the response of this class of teleconnections to large-scale warming? Key to understanding and forecasting future variations in growing season length.

**McCabe et al., 2015 \- Variability in the start, end, and length of frost-free periods across the conterminous United States during the past century**

* Key Points  
  * Only weak correlation exists between frost-free season start/end dates and climate indices  
  * Smallest frost-free season length variability found in north-central CONUS, greatest variability found in southern CONUS & coastal areas  
    * Potentially explained by more variable daily minimum temperatures during colder months and the impact that makes on sites whose frost-free seasons begin and end in colder months (i.e. southern & coastal states)  
  * April temperatures were strongly related to last spring frost, and October temperatures were strongly related to first fall frost  
    * \[What are the major drivers of specifically April and October temperatures in CONUS and can they be correlated to growing season length variability?\]  
  * Only 101 of 523 (19%) of sites indicate correlation between last spring frost and first fall frost  
    * Processes determining these dates largely unrelated  
  * Sites that see negative trends in first fall frost (earlier frost)  located in central CONUS  
    * Corresponds to ‘warming hole’ in same region  
    * Similar region in southeast CONUS not indicated in trends (addressed in limitations)  
  * Frost dates can vary widely in short distances due to terrain or surface vegetation impacts  
  * Correlations between detrended last spring / first fall frost dates and seasonal mean climate indices do not indicate anything more than weak correlations.  
    * Correlations for AMO are most significant  
      * When AMO is positive, first fall frost dates are later than average  
* Procedure (inc. Pros/Cons)  
  * GHCN Minimum Temperature data used, with 0°C inclusive used as threshold  
  * 654 sites met criteria of availability of data from 1920-2012, missing data filled in with regression approach  
  * Sites with frost-free seasons \<30 days or \>335 days removed  
    * 523 sites analyzed in total  
  * Performed correlation analysis between last spring / first fall frost dates at each station with NINO3.4, PNA, PDO, NAO, AO, and AMO.  
    * Detrended all data (removed linear trends) so correlations would not be impacted by long-term trends in data  
* Limitations  
  * Station coverage too sparse to indicate delay in last spring frost found in southeastern US due to ‘warming hole’ present in the region  
* Conclusions  
  * For most sites in CONUS, trend is towards earlier last spring frost and later first fall frost, causing longer frost-free seasons  
  * Last spring frost dates most significantly shifted earlier around 1983, while first fall frost rates most significantly shifted earlier around 1993  
  * Primary driver of variability is regional seasonal temperature variability, including both interannual and decadal variability  
* Open Questions  
  * Analysis indicates the need for research on dynamical processes that drive variability, particularly in relation to growing season length

---

**Conclusion**  
	All three papers agree on the basic tenet that an increase in mean hemispheric temperature lengthens the growing season in the contiguous United States. Additionally, the papers that also analyzed the correlation of climate indices with the variability in growing season length found that of the indices studied, only the AMO and PNA had statistically significant impacts, with the PNA having a slightly larger account for variability in the western U.S. and the AMO’s impact being less significant but more broadly observed across the country. This contradiction of the logical assumption that climate indices would be responsible for year-to-year variation in growing season length leads to potential research questions investigating other causes, such as investigating the impact of seasonal mean humidity and precipitable water on the beginning, end, or overall length of the growing season, or the impact of ocean heat content on coastal growing season variability. Other points of curiosity derived from these papers include utilizing other methods of defining the growing season temporally (such as atmospheric carbon readings) or investigating the correlation of  the atmospheric drivers of April and October temperature variation across the eastern U.S. to variability in growing season length in the region.  
If we choose to pursue these potential research questions and methods, I expect more reading would be necessary on topics such as atmospheric moisture (and its interaction with solar radiation), drivers of the eastern U.S.’s spring and fall seasonal weather patterns, and the ocean’s impact on coastal climates, as well as work to understand the timing of photosynthesis’s impact on atmospheric carbon readings each spring in relation to the time of the last spring frost.
