**Dobson et al. \- The influence of Lake Erie on changes temperature and frost dates**

* Key Points  
  * Later FFD due to Lake Erie sustaining warm temperatures throughout the year, causing coastal regions to stay warmer for longer.  
  * Lake mitigates extreme temperature events for coastal regions  
    * Author hypothesizes inland region’s FFD is changing at a faster rate due to less mitigation from the lake  
  * Inland areas experienced later frost date breakpoints than coastal areas  
    * Likely due to lake retaining increasingly warm temperatures earlier than surrounding air, so coastal areas experienced effects of warming earlier  
  * Data set shows FFD breakpoint beginning 4-7 years earlier than LFD breakpoint, which counters previous research (McCabe et al. 2015\)  
    * \[Is the spring last frost date not predictive at all? Is either season predictive for inland or coastal areas or are no seasonal frost breakpoints predictive of the other at all?\]  
  * Results demonstrate how regions in close proximity may experience large differences in effects of climate change due to local microclimates.  
* Procedure (inc. Pros/Cons)  
  * Data from 1948-2017 was taken from 13 weather stations spanning the southern region of Lake Erie  
    * 6 coastal stations within 10 miles of the lake  
    * 7 inland stations within between 30 and 50 miles of the lake  
  * Determined first fall frost date (FFD) and last spring frost date (LFD) for all stations for each year using the 0°C inclusive threshold  
  * Linear mixed-effects model was used to determine whether there was a significant difference between the coastal and inland regions for the date of first frost.  
  * Examined yearly maximum, minimum, and average temperatures at each station  
  * Breakpoints in data were used to determine how average FFD, LFD, FFS, and average temperature have changed  
* Conclusions  
  * Average FFD is significantly later for coastal regions than for inland regions  
    * 15-20 day difference in FFD between coastal and inland regions  
    * Rate of change greater for inland regions  
  * No significant difference in LFD between coastal regions and inland regions  
  * Rate of increase in FFS not significantly different between inland and coastal regions  
  * Both regions have decreasing maximum temperatures  
  * Inland regions appear to have colder minimum temperatures than coastal regions, but not a significant difference. The rate of increase is also not significantly different aside from yearly average, where coastal regions have a greater rate of increase.  
* Open Questions  
  * Is the Great Lakes’ microclimate altering the changes in temperature and frost dates? \[what portion of the difference does it account for? What would be the other factors between the inland and coastal sites?\]  
  * Future studies would benefit from data on lake temperatures  
  * Future studies on how climate changes affect ecosystems may benefit from consideration of how microclimates may affect the effects of climate change.

**Brown et al. 2026 \- Tropicalization of the Temperate Zone: Spatiotemporal Variability of Winter Warming and Declining Freeze Days Across the United States**

* Key Points  
  * Largest percentage losses in freeze days occurred across low-elevation, low-relieve regions at lower latitudes.  
  * Winter has warmed more than other seasons  
  * In arid regions, winter Tmax warming exceeded Tmin warming, consistent with moisture-limited area where less cloud cover increases daytime heating but nighttime cooling remains efficient  
  * ENSO exerts a strong influence on winter precipitation and Tmax across southern CONUS, but winter Tmin is less sensitive to ENSO  
  * Cold air outbreaks dominate coldest Tmin metric, may offset long-term warming observed in seasonally averaged metrics  
    * East of 85°W, coldest Tmax generally warmed (though not significantly), likely reflecting fewer cold air outbreaks  
  * Relative freeze-day losses are largest in warmer, lower latitude areas where freeze days are less frequent. Cooler areas experienced larger absolute declines but smaller relative losses.  
  * Land cover (especially the urban heat island effect) also influences surface temperature and may contribute to variability in the poleward retreat of freeze days  
* Procedure (inc. Pros/Cons)  
  * Defined the TTTz (tropical-temperate transition zone) between 25° \-38°N and 75°-107°W  
  * Created daily, county-level time series for temperature  
  * Annual temp values computed by averaging daily temps  
  * Winter temp averages were the Dec/Jan/Feb average of daily temps  
  * Coldest Tmin and Tmax days selected with coldest county-mean day for each value  
  * Freeze days analyzed seasonally to avoid splitting over calendar year  
* Conclusions  
  * 79% of land area observed statistically significant warming to annual average Tmax while no county observed significant cooling trends  
  * 99% of land area observed statistically significant warming to annual average Tmin  
  * 53% of land area observed significant warming in avg winter Tmax while no county observed significant cooling trends  
  * 70% of land area observed significant warming in winter average Tmin  
  * 70% of land area observed greater rates of winter warming in Tmin than in Tmax  
  * 85% of land area observed significant decrease in freeze days  
    * 80% of counties with \>10 freeze days observed contraction in concentration of freeze days temporally  
  * 327 counties showed significant trends towards later starts of their freeze day concentration periods  
  * 735 counties showed significant trends towards earlier end dates of their freeze day concentration periods  
  * Winter cold extreme events will continue, but may become less frequent and/or intense. Winter temperatures may become less variable and warmer  
* Open Questions  
  * Potential consequence of Arctic Amplification is reduction and moderation of cold air advection into midlatitudes, meaning milder cold extremes, but debate remains over whether this will increase or decrease cold extremes across N. America

**Ma et al. 2025 \- Increased Precipitation promotes aboveground biomass primarily by extending the growing season rather than by affecting leaf traits in alpine desert grasslands**

* Key Points  
  * Increased Precipitation led to earlier green-up date and later end to the growing season, significantly extending growing season length  
    * Longer growing season leads to longer growth period leading to higher biomass accumulation, though this effect may vary by ecosystem  
    * Earlier green-up date and longer growing season length induced by increased precipitation do not inherently imply low-temperature (frost) damage (though in certain ecosystems that is possible)  
    * Increased precipitation counters adverse effects of drought early in the growing season  
* Procedure (inc. Pros/Cons)  
  * 2 year manipulative experiment where precipitation at each rainfall event was increased 100%, 200%, 300%, and 400% for test planting areas in the alpine desert steppe of the Tibetan Plateau  
* Conclusions  
  * Increased precipitation led to earlier green-up date and later end of growing season of *S. glareosa* (test plant) in the two years the experiment was run  
    * All increased precipitation significantly extended the growing season length, but no significant difference was found between increased precipitation gradients  
      * Larger increases in precipitation input likely exceeded water-holding capacity of sandy soils in desert steppe  
    * Significant interannual differences in green-up dates, end of growing season, and growing season length

**Cornish et al. 2026 \- Diverse Indicators of Climate Change for New Jersey and Vicinity**

* Key Points  
  * In the U.S. Mid-Atlantic, surface temperature warming has been associated with greater nearby ocean temperatures and reduced the number of days dipping below freezing  
  * Surface temperature warming has also lengthened the climatological growing season by 13.4 days across the U.S. Northeast  
  * Average increase in frequency of extremely warm minimum temperatures across all stations was greater than increase in extremely hot maximum temperature events  
    * Minimum temperature warming more rapidly than maximum is suspected to be caused by increase in atmospheric moisture associated with warming that affects cloud cover and elevated soil moisture content from associated precipitation which can work to keep nighttime temperature higher.  
    * Warming northern U.S. Atlantic coastal waters have also been shown to elevate surface air temperatures of U.S. Northeast, potentially enhancing humidity and heat retention  
* Procedure (inc. Pros/Cons)  
  * Climatological and phenological observations were used to contextualize climate trends across multiple indicators  
  * Each dataset was processed into a representative measure per year and analyzed via a linear trend analysis  
  * Dates of first leaf and first bloom were converted into Julian days (days from 1/1 of each yr) and trends of these dates were analyzed by linear regression for each station  
* Conclusions  
  * Analysis provides evidence of local climate change in form of increased extreme heat days with decreased extreme cold days, higher coastal SSTs, decreases in lake ice duration, less severe winters, and earlier onsets of phenological spring  
* Limitations  
  * Spatial and temporal coverage of datasets used in study was incomplete, and the level of analysis complexity was relatively basic  
  * Difficult to isolate the effects of climate-change driven warming from urban heat island effects  
* Open Questions  
  * Author suggests further work to:  
    * Determine which months or timeframes during the year are driving observed changes in the indicators used  
    * Incorporate stations all the way from Philadelphia to NYC to better understand challenges posed to major urban areas by climate change  
    * Differentiate changes in local warming due to climate change from urbanization-related changes  
    * Expand on work by analyzing how atmospheric circulation patterns have changed, how these changes can influence indicator response, and how they might respond to further radiative forcing with continued warming  
    * Consider more indicators of relevance to specific sectors within New Jersey

**Hudson et al. 2022 \- Length of growing season is modulated by Northern Hemisphere jet stream variability**

* Key Points  
  * NHJ variability has been observed and is projected to increase due to anthropogenic warming  
  * For nearly a third of the Northern Hemisphere (NH), length of growing season (LOS) was impacted by Northern Hemisphere Jet (NHJ) position  
  * Changes in plant phenological patterns have potential to affect carbon, energy, and water cycles through changes in albedo and latent heat flux  
  * Preliminary analysis showed that 20% of variability in U.S. spring leaf-out was influenced by spring NHJ over the U.S. (Belmecheri et al. 2017\)  
  * Relative influence of different climate parameters may vary but temp and precip variability across all seasons is impacted by position of the NHJ  
* Procedure (inc. Pros/Cons)  
  * Used NHJ indices developed by Belmecheri et al. 2017, who identified 32 longitudinal sectors of NHJ coherence over four seasons  
  * Northern NHJ position typically corresponds to higher geopotential heights at 500 hPa, warmer temps, and drier soil north of mean NHJ latitude  
  * Southern NHJ position typically corresponds to lower geopotential heights at 500 hPa, cooler temps, and wetter soil south of mean NHJ latitude  
  * Focused analysis on LOS, start of season (SOS), and end of season (EOS) phenophases, measured in days of year (DOY)  
    * LOS calculated as difference between SOS and EOS  
  * Removed any trend in temperature over 32 year period at each phenoogy observation location using linear regressions  
* Conclusions  
  * Across NH, ecosystems experienced significant lengthening or shortening of LOS corresponding with anomalous positions of the NHJ in spring or fall  
    * Influence through impacts on surface meteorology at key times of growing season (influences seasonally relevant limiting factors on growth)  
  * Northern positions affected more regions than southern positions  
    * Northern position in spring typically corresponded with longer LOS  
    * Northern position in fall typically corresponded with shorter LOS (likely due to interaction of temperature and water availability)  
  * Fall northern NHJ anomalies produced more significant responses in LOS than spring northern anomalies  
* Limitations  
  * Unable to capture affects of moisture on phenology, except when moisture is correlated with temperature  
* Open Questions  
  * This analysis focused on temperature and water availability, but other climatic factors, such as insolation levels also potentially influence plant senescence (state or process of dying)
